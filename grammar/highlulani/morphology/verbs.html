Verbs

A verb is a necessary part of any sentence, except for copular sentences (q.v.).

Basic Verbs

The class of basic verbs is semi-closed. Basic verbs consist of one stem.

’i to say

dissu to feel

’usa to see

janni to move

kissa to fight

miku to make red

Compound Verbs

In order to extend or specify the meaning of many verbs, verbs can be compounded much as a noun can. Compound verbs are, however, much more common than compound nouns, to make up for the relative lack of basic verbs.

’isinna to tell a story (’i to speak + sinna story)

dissu’ahati to be happy (dissu to feel + ’ahati happiness)

pamilami to be conscious (pa think + milami surroundings)

Monosyllabic verbs may only be used this way. The object of an transitive monosyllabic verb can be used to form the compound, however, this does not occur with polysyllabic verbs.

dissu ’ahati to feel happiness (as above)

gasunu to put the cloak (somewhere) (ga to put + sunu cloak)

*ga sunu

*jannisunu

janni sunu to move the cloak (janni move)


Transitivity

Verbs are broken down into one of a number of types:

Transitive verbs require an object.

Intransitive verbs do not require an object.

Ambitransitive verbs can be used transitively or intransitively.

The transitivity of a compound verb is the same as that of the base verb.
